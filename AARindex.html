Abstract

Our mission is to address agricultural wastewater through an eco-innovative filter using biochar and zeolite. Using real-time sensors, we evaluated changes in turbidity, TDS, and pH. Results confirmed a strong improvement in water quality, reinforcing our project's impact on sustainable agriculture.

Introduction

Modern farming contributes significantly to water pollution, especially through runoff rich in fertilizers and pesticides. Most rural areas lack access to water purification systems. This project focuses on creating a low-cost, natural, sensor-assisted filtration unit to treat this wastewater for reuse in irrigation.

Methods

We assembled a layered filter containing biochar (to absorb organic pollutants) and zeolite (to trap heavy metals). A microcontroller collected readings from water quality sensors placed before and after the filter. We ran multiple cycles to assess consistency in water purification results.

Results

Water tested after filtration showed remarkable improvement: TDS reduced by 75%, turbidity lowered by 90%, and pH levels shifted to near neutral. Graphs showed repeatable patterns over three separate trials. These results demonstrate the filter’s potential scalability and reliability in agricultural settings.

Analysis

The analysis revealed a clear correlation between the filtration time and the degree of water purity achieved. Longer filtration times yielded better results, especially in turbidity and TDS reduction. Sensor feedback graphs were used to visualize changes dynamically. The filtration performance was consistent across all tests, validating our material choice and filter design. We also noticed that biochar was more effective for organic waste, while zeolite was essential in neutralizing harmful ions. The system’s modularity allows it to be scaled up for different usage capacities.

Conclusion

The system offers a sustainable solution for wastewater treatment in agriculture. It is affordable, effective, and scalable. Our approach shows how combining simple materials with modern electronics can solve environmental problems in resource-limited communities.
